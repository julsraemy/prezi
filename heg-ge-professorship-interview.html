Hi everyone! In today's lesson, we will explore how information professionals engage with artificial intelligence across a range of activities. Specifically, we will look at how individuals and institutions in the information science field can work with, design with, and develop for AI.

This AI evolution spans over five decades, from foundational logic systems and relational databases in the 1970s through rule-based expert systems in the 1980s-90s, database revolution and early semantic web technologies (RDF, OWL, SPARQL) in the 2000s, neural networks and deep learning alongside SHACL validation in the 2010s, to today's large language models with RAG systems and semantic web 1.2 updates. Each era brought new possibilities and challenges, shaping how we think about AI's role in information work.

Core applications are transforming practice: multi-language analysis breaks down global access barriers, automated metadata generation dramatically reduces cataloging backlogs, and conversational interfaces democratize collection discovery for non-expert users. However, the reality is more nuanced than the hype suggests. Issues like algorithmic bias, privacy concerns, and vendor dependence create tension between AI's potential and practical implementation. Information professionals must balance automation benefits with human oversight, ethical responsibilities, and budget constraints. I will now give you a few AI implementatsions and uses cases done in the information science field.

The VSA-AAS - The Association of Swiss Archivists - white paper represents thinking about AI implementation in archives. There is for instance the concept of "fluid metadata" where information is seen as dynamic rather than static. The emphasis on human-machine collaboration reflects this argument that AI should help rather than replace human capabilities. The white paper's focus on "depth indexing" demonstrates how AI can create new layers of meaning within the infosphere while preserving essential human judgment about context and evidence value.

ETH Zurich Library's E-Periodica Next Level Access project demonstrates large-scale implementation of AI for cultural heritage. The project uses machine learning for named entity recognition and vector embeddings to semantically match and link millions of person references to authority files like the Gemeinsame Normdatei. While the scale is impressive, the project emphasises local, resource-efficient solutions rather than relying on commercial AI services. This approach allows the library to build internal expertise, understand AI limitations, and maintain control over their data processing workflows. The project exemplifies the Collections as Data approach, making historical periodicals computationally accessible for research. - 10 million digitised pages processed - 40 million person entity references extracted - 22 million names identified and linked - 800,000 connections to external authority files (GND)

The IIIF Tsukushi Viewer developed by the Center for Open Data in the Humanities in Japan, leverages IIIF standards to connect multimodal AI models with classical Japanese texts. The demo showed how users can select specific pages or regions, ask questions in modern Japanese about classical content, and receive contextual responses that bridge the gap between historical documents and contemporary understanding.

Annif represents a mature approach to automated subject indexing that addresses the labor-intensive nature of manual cataloging. Originally developed using metadata from Finland's Finna.fi discovery interface as training corpus, Annif is an open-source toolkit that combines multiple natural language processing and machine learning algorithms. After training with institutional subject vocabularies and existing metadata, it can suggest subject headings for new documents. Annif builds on individual algorithm strengths while adapting to different institutional settings

These examples span different approaches to AI implementation, though many are still in pilot phases. Yet we're still in early stages of moving beyond simple tool adoption. The reality is that running comprehensive AI-integrated systems requires substantial ongoing investment in infrastructure, training, and maintenance that many institutions cannot yet afford.

This analysis illustrates how AI engagement in information science is multifaceted, but we must also acknowledge a growing crisis. The exercise cards demonstrate that successful AI implementation requires professionals who can work across these boundaries. However, the aggressive data harvesting by AI companies is now threatening the very infrastructure that makes open access possible. We face the challenge of distinguishing truth from fiction in an increasingly complex information landscape. The current bot surge has forced heritage institutions to implement blocking measures that contradict their open access missions. Libraries and archives are spending vast amounts of time and resources on bot defence rather than improving services for human users. This creates a paradox: while AI has the potential to improve access to information, its demand for training data can impede progress.

Information specialists should serve as important mediators between AI systems and users, requiring enhanced evaluation skills to assess AI outputs. Standards like IIIF and Linked Data enable AI interoperability, whilst quality metadata serves as training data for ML systems. Professionals face a dual challenge: harnessing AI's potential whilst protecting the open access infrastructure that aggressive data harvesting threatens to destroy. This requires both technical bot management solutions and policy advocacy for responsible AI development. Rather than facing extinction, information professionals are experiencing transformation — AI handles routine tasks whilst humans focus on high-level analysis, critical evaluation, and services requiring domain expertise that goes beyond pattern recognition.

Différentes perspectives ou thématiques de recherche que je peux apporter à la HEG de Genève, différents mandats, formations continues ou projets de recherche et là comme l'exercice l'exige, je vais vous parler d'un projet de recherche que j'aimerais amener ici.

Plus concrètement ce serait une suite d'un projet qui se nomme The Flow - ou en tout cas qui se baserait fortement sur les résultats de celui-ci. The Flow est une collaboration entre l'Université de Berne, l'Université de Bielefeld ainsi que le Musée européen de la Hanse teutonique à Lübeck. Ce projet de recherche durera jusqu'en juin 2026.

Quatre sous-projets contribuent à l'objectif global du projet en analysant des sources juridiques et administratives couvrant différentes périodes et régions, notamment l'Angleterre (XIIIe-XIVe siècles), la région hanséatique d'Europe du Nord (XIVe-XVIIe siècles), la Suisse (XVIe-XVIIIe siècles) et l'Éthiopie (XIXe siècle).

Le projet Flow a démontré le potentiel des technologies HTR et des flux numériques pour l'analyse des corpus historiques. Confluence représente la maturation de ces innovations : transformer les workflow expérimentaux en systèmes de production interopérables au sein au sein des systèmes de bibliothèques et d'archives existants grâce aux standards IIIF, au Linked Data et via la génération augmentée par récupération (ou RAG - Retrieval Augmented Generation). Le processus "GLAM-in-the-loop" préserve l'expertise humaine de professionnels de bibliothèques et d'archives dans la boucle décisionnelle.

Si The Flow a démontré - et continue de démontrer - le potentiel de l'intelligence artificielle pour l'analyse de textes historiques, la plupart des résultats restent confinés dans des silos de recherche. Les institutions chargées du patrimoine culturel ont besoin que ces capacités soient intégrées dans leurs flux de travail quotidiens, leurs systèmes de catalogage et leurs interfaces utilisateur, passant ainsi de « l'IA pour la recherche » à « l'IA pour les institutions chargées du patrimoine culturel ». L'émergence des technologies RAG et des écosystèmes IIIF matures rend désormais cette intégration institutionnelle possible. À l'instar des affluents qui se jettent dans un grand cours d'eau, The Confluence rassemble des agents IA spécialisés (reconnaissance de texte, contextualisation, RAG et systèmes GLAM-in-the-loop) au sein d'un écosystème auquel les institutions du patrimoine culturel peuvent plus facilement adopter et maintenir.

Confluence aurait un budget de 2,5M CHF sur 4 ans via le fonds national suisse (FNS). Équipe de direction interdisciplinaire : sciences de l'information (HEG Genève), humanités numériques (Berne) et histoire (Zurich) - collaboration déjà établie et éprouvée. Partenaires stratégiques incluent des plateformes patrimoniales suisses (e-manuscripta, e-codices) et internationales (IIIF, Europeana) - ce qui garantirait l'impact et l'adoption à large échelle.

Confluence s'organise autour de 5 work packages complémentaires sur 4 ans : - WP0 assure la coordination générale - WP1 (24 mois) développe l'agent de reconnaissance textuelle - base technique du projet - WP2-3-4 (36 mois chacun) couvrent contextualisation, découverte et intégration institutionnelle - L'approche modulaire permet un développement parallèle et des livrables échelonnés - WP4 garantit l'adoption durable par les institutions partenaires
